What Even Is PowerPoint? 🤔

🎞️

A Presentation App

PowerPoint lets you build a deck of slides to share information visually. Each slide is like a page — but designed to be shown on a screen to an audience.

🌍

Used Everywhere

School assignments, business pitches, science conferences, TED Talks — if someone's presenting to a group, there's a good chance slides are involved.

🎨

Design Matters

Bad slides confuse people. Good slides support what you're saying. Today you'll learn the rules that separate the two.

📁

Files = Presentations

A PowerPoint file is called a presentation. It contains multiple slides. Save it as .pptx and store it in your OneDrive folder.

Key Terms — Know These 📍

Slide
One page of your presentation

Each slide appears one at a time when you present. Keep them clean — one idea per slide.

Placeholder
Click to add title

Dotted boxes on a slide. Click one to type. They keep your layout consistent across slides.

Slide Layout
Title Slide / Title + Content

A preset arrangement of placeholders. Pick a layout that suits your content — don't start from scratch every time.

Theme / Design
Design tab → Themes

A set of matching colours and fonts applied to every slide at once. Way faster than styling each slide manually.

Transition
Fade, Push, Wipe…

The animation between slides when you click to advance. Use sparingly — one subtle transition for the whole deck is enough.

Animation
Appear, Fly In, Fade…

Motion applied to individual elements on a slide. Text can fly in, images can fade. Easy to overdo — less is more.

Presenter View
Slide Show → Presenter View

Shows your notes and next slide on your screen while the audience only sees the current slide. Use it when presenting.

Speaker Notes
Notes panel below each slide

Your cue cards — type what you want to say here. The audience never sees them, only you do in Presenter View.

Design Rules — Good vs Bad 🎨

✅ Do this

One idea per slide

If you have three points, make three slides. Cramming everything onto one slide overwhelms your audience.

Slide 1: Point A  →  Slide 2: Point B  →  Slide 3: Point C
❌ Never do this

Wall of text

If your slide has more than 6 lines of text, you're writing an essay, not a slide. Cut it down. Use dot points.

Nobody reads 8 sentences on a slide. They'll read it instead of listening to you.
💡 Pro tip

The 6×6 rule

Maximum 6 dot points per slide, maximum 6 words per dot point. Forces you to keep it tight.

✓ "Save to OneDrive before leaving"
✗ "You need to make sure that you remember to save your file to your OneDrive folder before the end of class"
⚠️ Watch out

Font chaos

Max 2 fonts per presentation. One for headings, one for body text. Mixing 4 different fonts looks unprofessional.

Heading font: bold, large  ·  Body font: clean, readable
✅ Do this

High contrast text

Dark text on a light background, or light text on a dark background. Never light on light or dark on dark.

White text on dark blue ✓  ·  Yellow text on white ✗
❌ Never do this

Animation overload

Every element spinning, flying, and bouncing is distracting. One simple transition for the whole deck. That's it.

Fade or Push = professional  ·  Spiral + Bounce + Flash = chaos

The Rules That Actually Matter ⭐

⭐ Golden Rules

1

Slides support your speaking — they're not a script. If you're reading every word off the slide, you're doing it wrong.

2

One idea per slide. If you're unsure, split it into two slides.

3

Pick a theme and stick with it. Consistency looks professional. Changing colours and fonts on every slide looks chaotic.

4

Images must be relevant. A random clip-art fish on a slide about climate change just confuses people.

5

Save to OneDrive — Ctrl+S — before you close the app. Every time.

6

Practice out loud before you present. Knowing your slides is not the same as knowing your talk.

When Things Go Wrong 🚨

🚨 Common Mistakes

Too much text

Fix: Cut each dot point to 6 words or less. Move the detail into your speaker notes instead.

Font too small

Fix: Minimum 24pt for body text, 36pt+ for headings. If it looks big on your laptop, it's probably right on a projector.

Images look blurry

Fix: Use high-resolution images. Don't stretch a small image to fill a slide — find a bigger one instead.

Slideshow won't start

Fix: Press F5 or go to Slide Show tab → From Beginning. Make sure you're not in a text box when you press it.

File not saved

Fix: Ctrl+S right now. Check it's saved to OneDrive → Year 7 → DigiTech → Classwork, not just locally.
